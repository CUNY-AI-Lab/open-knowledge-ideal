I Part I

What AI Is

How these models work, and how they’re made

Familiar tools

The chatbots you know

ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Gemini. The basic experience is familiar: you type something, and it types back.

How it works

Predicting the next piece of text

When the model answers, it generates a likely continuation of your text, one piece at a time, rather than retrieving a stored fact.

It repeats that step to produce each answer.

Tokens
Your text is split into word-sized pieces
Numbers
Pieces become numbers the model can do math on
Weighing
It weighs which earlier words matter most
Next piece, then repeat
Pick a likely next piece, then do it again
A caution

Confidently wrong

Because it’s built to produce what sounds right, it can sound completely sure and still be wrong.

It invents things

Citations and quotes that never existed, written as confidently as the true ones.

It carries its patterns

Including the skews in the text it learned from, which show up unevenly in what it produces.

Models are trained in a way that rewards a confident guess over an honest “I don’t know.” Source: Kalai et al. (OpenAI & Georgia Tech), 2025.

How it’s made

How a model learns

It learns to guess the next piece by reading an enormous amount of text and adjusting itself, over and over, to guess a little better. Afterward, people tune it with feedback on which answers are better.

That text is scraped at scale from across the public web, books and code included, very likely with open materials like the ones you make.

So where the training text came from, and who agreed to it, is a question we come back to in the worries.

A concrete example

Inside The Pile

Models rarely say what they trained on. The Pile is an exception: an 825 GB corpus EleutherAI assembled and documented openly in 2020, so we can see what goes into a model.

It bundles 22 sources. Most are what an open-knowledge project would celebrate. One, Books3, is what the lawsuits are about. That puts open work and pirated books in the same pile.

On the corpus: Gao et al., “The Pile,” EleutherAI, 2020; on the Books3 takedown, Wikipedia, 2023.

II Part II

What It Can Do Now

Reaching past its training, and what to check

The problem & the fix

From a guess to a grounded answer

Ask a plain model for today’s weather and it makes a confident guess, with no live data behind it and no way to reach today’s forecast.

Newer systems can reach outside themselves with a few tools:

  • Search the web or a catalog
  • Read a file you give it
  • Run code and read the result

← Click to compare the guess with the grounded answer.

Ungrounded
A plausible guess from patterns, not today’s forecast.
Grounded
After searching live sources, it reports current conditions.
Agentic

Working in a loop

A plain chatbot answers once and stops there.

An agentic one keeps going. It looks at a result, decides what to do next, calls another tool, and repeats until the task seems done.

So it can take several steps toward a goal on its own.

III Part III

The Common Worries

The concerns, and what we’d add

The worry that’s ours

What happens to “open”

Open licensing rests on a bargain. You may reuse the work freely, as long as you credit the source and give back to the commons. AI training takes the reuse and gives nothing back.

The more openly you license, the easier your work is to ingest. Paywalled writing had some friction protecting it; openly licensed work has none.

The first reflex is to lock it down or add a no-AI clause. Locking down would shrink the commons faster than the scrapers do. Researchers call that a “data winter.”

On openness as its own vulnerability: Open Future, “The Paradox of Open”; Verhulst, “The Weaponisation of Openness,” 2025.

The open bargain
Reuse freely · credit the source · give back
AI training ingests it

A common worry · close to home

AI eats the audience

The concern

Even when people keep sharing openly, AI answers mean fewer of them ever reach the source. Referral traffic to open sites has fallen hard over the past year, and Wikipedia’s human visits are dropping even as the bots scraping it climb.

What we’d add

For a commons, readers are the contributor pool. Someone spots an error, clicks edit, and stays. Fewer readers means fewer editors, so the commons loses the attention and the volunteer labor that keep it alive, a decline quieter than enclosure and harder to reverse.

On the referral collapse: AdExchanger, 2025; on Wikipedia’s human traffic and crawler load, Wikimedia, 2025.

A common worry · close to home

Sold over your head

The concern

Your own scholarship is being licensed to AI firms without your say. The publishers that hold the copyright signed the deals: Taylor & Francis took $10 million from Microsoft, Wiley $44 million. By the Authors Guild’s account, no authors were asked and none were paid.

What we’d add

The work that got sold was the closed, paywalled kind, where you signed copyright over to a publisher. So “openness is the vulnerability” is only half the story. Handing your work to a closed gatekeeper didn’t protect it either. A few presses now ask first; most don’t.

On the publisher–AI licensing deals: The Bookseller, 2025; Authors Guild.

Where that leaves you

You can’t license your way out

Many people’s first move is to add a no-AI or non-commercial clause. Creative Commons, the steward of these licenses, says that doesn’t work and backfires.

The courts ruled it legal

In 2025, courts ruled the training itself is fair use, even for copyrighted work. Anthropic still paid $1.5 billion for downloading those books from pirate sites, though the courts allowed the training itself.

Locking it down backfires

Because training is often already fair use, “the CC license conditions have limited application to machine reuse.” A restrictive license mainly blocks the people who would legitimately reuse your work, and does nothing to stop the model.

What’s working instead is collective. Creative Commons’ CC signals, its 2026 turn toward shared infrastructure and conditional access, Wikimedia metering scrapers behind a paid API. All of it is nascent and contested. We use these tools, and we don’t think this is solved. It’s the first thing we want to discuss.

CC licenses can’t block training: CC legal primer, 2025. The ruling: Bartz v. Anthropic + $1.5B settlement. Collective responses: CC, “From Signals to Infrastructure,” 2026; Wikimedia paid API, 2025.

A common worry · close to home

Open vs closed weights

The concern

Build an OER on a closed, API-only model and the content can carry an open license, but the model underneath stays proprietary. No one can retain, run, inspect, or redistribute the model the way OER’s 5 R’s require, and using it takes an account and a subscription. So you reintroduce the cost and gatekeeping that open education set out to remove.

What we’d add

Open-weight models can be downloaded and run locally, which makes the 5 R’s possible again, and they’ve nearly caught up on capability. Two things to watch, though. “Open weights” is not “open source”: most withhold the training data and code, under use-restricting licenses like Llama, and the OSI’s bar of data, code, and weights is cleared by few, such as OLMo and Pythia. And open weights still need hardware to run, so without shared compute the openness can stay out of reach.

On the 5 R’s and what counts as open: Wiley, “Defining the ‘Open’”; on proprietary AI vs. open education, UNESCO, 2024; “open weights” ≠ “open source,” OSI, 2024; the compute divide, Society & AI, 2025.

A common worry · with an upside

The commons still has leverage

The concern

Train a model on the web’s growing pile of machine text and it degrades, an effect called model collapse. The rare cases drop out first, and adding more data doesn’t fix it. Most new web pages already carry AI-generated content.

What we’d add

That makes verified human work scarcer, and more valuable. The open commons you steward, checked and sourced and human, is something the machines can’t synthesize for themselves.

On model collapse: Shumailov et al., Nature, 2024.

Where this points

Toward public AI

If the problem is private enclosure of a public commons, one response is to build public infrastructure, the kind of AI that anyone can reach and that’s owned and governed in common. Some call it public libraries for AI. It’s early and underfunded, nowhere near the scale of the commercial models.

This lab is one small instance of it. It’s a public, non-commercial service that runs open-weight models and keeps nothing you send it.

On public AI and the commons: Open Future, “Public AI”; “AI as commons,” Internet Policy Review, 2025.

What to ask

Judging appropriate use

A few questions to ask in every case:

Accountable

A person stands behind the result, and it’s theirs to license once they’ve shaped it.

Disclosed

Say where AI helped. The lab has a disclosure tool for it.

Verified

Check what it produced against a reliable source before it ships.

Grounded

Hand it your own materials; don’t rely on it to already know.

An example

ThinkWith

A first-year writing instructor, Nicole Walker, built this after our workshops. A student pastes in a source passage and a cultural artifact, plus a paragraph connecting the two. The model gives feedback on how well the connection works.

What worked: the AI never writes the paragraph for the student. It keeps sending them back to revise until the paragraph ties the source and the artifact together.

Where it breaks: the judge is itself a model scoring a rubric, so the teacher still has to judge the result. And Walker said building it meant “committing all the sins of instrumentalist thinking.”

🔒 ailab.gc.cuny.edu/blog/thinkwith Open live ↗
Adopting or refusing

Informed refusal and informed adoption

Adopting and refusing are both legitimate. Whichever you choose, you should be able to say why.

Adopt, with reasons

Use it where it helps, with someone accountable for the result, and say why.

Refuse, with reasons

Decline it where it doesn’t fit, and say why. The GC guidance leaves room for exactly that.
